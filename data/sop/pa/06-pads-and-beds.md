# 06 · Worship Pads and Prayer Beds

**Purpose**: How to use ambient pads under prayer, transitions, and reflective moments — without distracting from what's being said or sung.
**Audience**: PA volunteer on duty.
**Last reviewed**: 2026-05-08 — Shine Zhang

---

## What pads/beds are (and aren't)

**Pads** = ambient, often instrument-free or very-light synth tracks in a key, designed to sit underneath spoken word or quiet music. Common uses:

- Under prayer
- Under altar call / response
- Between songs in a worship set (smooth transitions instead of dead silence)
- Under offering or communion

**They are not**:

- Background music for pre-service / post-service (that's a normal playlist on Ch 15/16)
- A replacement for an instrumentalist's parts
- Loud enough to draw attention

## How to play a pad

1. Identify the key (ask worship leader if not sure — they have the chord chart)
2. Open the pad file in QuickTime / VLC / iTunes (whatever's installed)
3. Set output to system audio (which routes through Ch 15/16)
4. Start playback **before** the moment it's needed — fade in, don't pop in
5. Bring the Ch 15/16 fader up gently (~30–40% of unity, depending on what's happening on top)
6. **Loop** the pad if it's not long enough (most pads are 5–10 min loops by design)

To end the pad cleanly:

1. Fade Ch 15/16 fader down over ~5 seconds at the end of the moment
2. Stop playback once faded out
3. Don't abrupt-cut unless the worship leader cues a hard transition

---

## When to NOT use a pad

- Under sermon (distracts from spoken word)
- Under loud, full-band worship (gets buried; adds nothing)
- During announcements (no — silence is fine)
- If the worship leader hasn't asked for one (default to silence; pads are intentional)

---

## Prayer-set transitions

The most common pattern at LSC:

1. Worship set ends on a song in some key (let's say D)
2. Pastor or worship leader transitions to prayer
3. Operator brings up the **D pad** smoothly as the song ends
4. Pad continues underneath the prayer
5. Pad fades out cleanly when prayer ends, before the next segment

Practice this transition at rehearsal. Getting it smooth is what separates "felt prayerful" from "felt like a meeting."
